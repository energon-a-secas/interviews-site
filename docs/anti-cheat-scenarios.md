# Anti-Cheat Interview Scenarios — Vibe Check

These scenarios show how an interviewer can use the anti-cheat observations during real interviews. Each scenario maps observable behavior to the corresponding observation control and follow-up probe.

## Scenario 1: The long-lag expert

**What you see:**
After a simple question, the candidate is silent for 8–10 seconds, then delivers a polished, jargon-heavy, perfectly structured answer.

**Tick:** `cheat-pace` → "Sudden shift" (0)

**Follow-up:**
> "That was a complete answer. Can you compress it into one sentence for me?"

**If they own it:** they can summarize.  
**If they don't:** they repeat the full monologue or stall.

## Scenario 2: The generic STAR machine

**What you see:**
Every answer follows Situation → Task → Action → Result with buzzwords but no names, dates, or emotions.

**Tick:** `cheat-template` → "Generic STAR" (0)

**Follow-up:**
> "Skip the setup. What exactly did you type or click in that moment?"

**If they own it:** they give file names, commands, or specific UI steps.  
**If they don't:** they stay at the summary level.

## Scenario 3: Tone/vocabulary whiplash

**What you see:**
The candidate's English is casual and hesitant, then suddenly becomes formal and verbose mid-answer.

**Tick:** `cheat-tone` → "Clear mismatch" (0)

**Follow-up:**
Ask the same question again in different words a few minutes later. Compare the vocabulary level and structure.

**If they own it:** both versions sound like the same person.  
**If they don't:** the second answer feels like a different author.

## Scenario 4: The second-screen reader

**What you see:**
On a video call, the candidate repeatedly glances down or to the side, types, or you hear mouse clicks before answering.

**Tick:** `cheat-device` → "Sustained" (0)

**Follow-up:**
> "Can you look at the camera while you walk me through that?"

Then ask an off-topic question to break any prepared text.

## Scenario 5: Off-topic curveball

**What you see:**
You deliberately ask something adjacent but off-script: "What did the room look like during that incident?" The candidate keeps answering the original question.

**Tick:** `cheat-offtopic` → "Falls apart" (0)

**Follow-up:**
> "I notice you answered my earlier question. Can you address the room/layout part?"

## Scenario 6: Echoing the prompt

**What you see:**
The candidate starts answers by repeating key words from your question verbatim, as if feeding a prompt back.

**Tick:** `cheat-echo` → "Often" (0)

**Follow-up:**
Rephrase the question several times and see if the echo persists.

## Cluster rule of thumb

- **0 red flags:** no special action.
- **1–2 red flags around the same topic:** probe deeper with the relevant category probes (Technical, Story, Ownership).
- **3+ red flags:** document in the internal report and consider a follow-up live interview with camera on, no screen share, and unscripted questions.

## What NOT to do

- A single signal is not proof of cheating. Use clusters.
- Never accuse the candidate during the interview. Use the section to inform your recommendation, not your tone.
- Keep anti-cheat notes out of the candidate feedback report.
